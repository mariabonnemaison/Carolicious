The Arepa: a Cultural Staple and Lifeline

By: Maria Bonnemaison

The humanitarian crisis in Venezuela has led to the departure of over 7 million people from the country. The massive exodus of Venezuelans has caused them to arrive in all corner of the world, and places where they have never settle before. Everywhere they go, they leave behind a cultural footprint, a piece of home that is uniquely theirs. For these migrants, the arepa has become a saving grace that has allowed them the opportunity to start a new life, leaving behind the hardships they faced at home.

Carolina Garcia immigrated to the United States in 2016 with her family and colleague Carolina Salinas. Together they opened their arepa bar, Carolicious, and the rest is history. Garcia and Salinas have shared their heritage in Boston which has welcomed them warmly. They came to Boston to give their families safety and freedom, but what they didnt expect was the scale of support for their restaurant in the city, a place that was unfamiliar with Venezuelan cuisine.




Other Venezuelans Restaurants in Greater Boston

Carolicious isn't the only Venezuelan business thriving in the city. Restaurants like Orinoco: a Latin kitchen have found their footing in Boston's South End, Brookline and Cambridge. Don Tequeño y Doña Arepa is serving customer in Roxbury/ Jamaica Plain. In West Roxbury, Viva Mi Arepa is also spreading Venezuelan cuisine.
